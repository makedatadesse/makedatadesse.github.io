Remembrance, Return, Remains

Conversations with the Self: Artist reflections + Notes from interviews

Artists within this exhibition were asked to reflect on their experiences of identity as first and second generation diaspora – in their youth, as young adults, and now, as artists. What came of this were conversations that revealed a shared practice of creation as a means of searching for the Self; family and community members become symbols: portals to access memory, nostalgia, and ancestry.

Elsa Lakew

  • Q: On displacement + migration, identity, and writing:
    • A: “A lot of my writing centers around my experience of migrating to the US. That experience was traumatizing for me as a kid and it colored so much of who I am today. It shaped some of my core wounds of abandonment and loss... a part of me is always trying to reconcile with that trauma, especially through my writing. I didn’t have the language to explain or even process what I was experiencing and feeling as a kid. And now that I have that ability, every time I allow myself to write about it, I feel like I’m giving voice to my inner child. It’s healing.”
  • Q: What environments have you felt most connected to your sense of placement?
    • A: "The environment I feel most connected to is my church. I grew up in the church, watching my mother sing as a member of the choir. It was a big part of my life growing up. The mezmur, the incense and the people there were always a grounding force for me."

Odaa Ethiopian Restaurant

  • Excerpts of reflection from March 2024 interview with Mr. Abraham Oluma
    • A1: “These images actualized, for me, the reality that there was a before of my community here too; the conflicts I faced in youth with feelings of loss (for traditions, connections to community, a time unknown), paired with a pressure to adapt to the dominant prominent culture as a means to succeed was intensified in this before."
    • A2: "...We spoke for nearly two hours, and in that time he recounted memories of hardship: racism and xenophobia, wavering bonds and allyship within (the incredibly small at the time) communities from the Horn as conflicts back home grew, and raising children with no true resources to pass on traditions."
    • A3: "...Mr. Oluma revealed that some years after its opening, they would begin to partner with a local American church to sponsor hundreds of refugees arriving from Somalia, Eritrea, and Ethiopia, providing some with work..."
    • A4: "...I thought of my parents, uncles, and aunties -- all those who had immigrated and moved, moved then settled...I thanked him for his role in nurturing this place where my parents were able to eventually find community some 10 years later. To him, his actions were not proof of philanthropy, rather part of the nature of protecting your people."

Nafyar

  • Q: How do you see the relationship between space + physicality and sense of identity?
    • A: “Space and physicality’s relationship to a sense of identity is very important, especially in this country. Owning property is an affirmation of one’s existence as a citizen of this country. As someone from a nomadic background, ownership is not as strict and codified as it is in this country. We understood our relationship to the land as unfixed constantly moving and in relation to it. When reading the stars to navigate upcoming storms and weather events. When constructing your “Guri” –the temporary home that nomadic Somalis would construct in relation to the land it was being constructed upon. The relationship we have with land is not one of property or ownership but rather of mutual understanding of temporality and survival. Space is no longer a fixed thing of ownership but rather it has its purpose and existence greater than we can imagine. I try to understand this relationship by allowing space to just exist on the canvas naturally, without trying to control the composition."

Addisalem Alemu

  • Q: If and when you long for it how do you find ways to be connected to your past/your culture?
  • Addis interviewed their cousin, Meron Alemu
    • A: “When I miss my culture and the comforts it used to offer me, I seek out the food. With my favorite dishes, I can feel at home without being home and it evokes a safe place to connect with my country. My past was filled with great meals and specific dishes bring me back to some of the most innocent times of my life. While they may be far and few between, those memories help me remember there is more than just discomfort and harm that has come from my past back home."
